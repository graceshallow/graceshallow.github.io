Why break down the two juries that each spent hours considering the guilt of Cosby?

Race is an undeniable factor in this case. Constand, a white woman, convicted Cosby, a Black man, of a crime. In a statement Camille Cosby released after her husband's verdict was announced, she likened the case to the fate of Emmett Till, a 14-year-old Black child who was murdered after a white woman accused him of harassment in a grocery store during the 1950s.

A 2012 study in The Quarterly Journal of Economics found that juries with no Black members are more likely to convict Black defendants than those with Black members.

White defendants, overall, are convicted at a lesser rate than Black defendants.

Though, one must take into account that the county from which jurors were pulled for both of these trials has a majority of white residents. Only 9.6 percent of Montgomery County's population is Black, according to the U.S. Census Bureau.
